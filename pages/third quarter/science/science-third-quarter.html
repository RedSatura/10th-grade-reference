3.1: Human Reproduction

Human Reproduction
Humans begin their lives as a single cell. This is formed by the fusion of two sex cells - an egg cell and a sperm cell - from the parents.
As humans reach adolescence, their sex organs mature and undergo changes to be able to produce sex cells needed to produce new individuals.
These sex cells contain structures called chromosomes which contain genetic infromation.

3.1.1: Male Reproductive System

Male Reproductive System
The male reproductive system produces sperm cells and delivers them inside the female reproductive system.
In males, the primary reproductive organs are the testes.
Testes
The testes are oval-shaped organs found inside a protective sac of skin called the scrotum.
The location of the testes in the scrotum is very important for the proper development of sperm.
Since the scrotum lies outside the abdominal cavity, its temperature is one to three degrees lower than the ideal body temperature.
This makes it ideal for sperm production, which requires a lower temperature.
Male Hormones
To regulate the function of the testes, two hormones are secreted by the anterior pituitary gland found in the brain.
The luteinizing hormone stimulated the secretion of the sex hormone testosterone.
The follicle-stimulating hormone, along with testosterone, stimulates sperm production.
Testosterone is responsible for the development of secondary male characteristics during puberty such as deepening of the voice and growth of facial hair.
Epididymis
At the onset of puberty, teenage boys start to produce sperm cells in their testes.
Thereafter, an adult male produces several hundred million sperm celss per day.
After being produced in the seminiferous tubules, the sperms mature and travel to the epydidymis, where they will mature until they are capable of swimming and moving.
The epydidymis is also where most sperms are stored.
Mature sperms cells travel from the epydidymis to the vas deferens, then exit the body through the urethra.
The Human Sperm Cell
A human sperm cell consists of three parts: a head that contains the chromosomes, a midpiece that contains the mitochondria, and a long tail that is responsible for movement.
The head is made up of a nucleus covered by a cap called the acrosome, which stores the enzymes needed to penetrate an egg cell during fertilization.
The midpiece contains mitochondria that supplies the energy needed to propel the sperm through the female reproductive tract.
The tail of the sperm is a powerful flagellum that enables the sperm cell to move.
Delivery of the Sperm
The urethra leads the sperm cell through the penis, the male organ that deposits the sperm in the female reproductive system during sexual intercourse.
During sexual excitation, blood rushes into the penis, making it rigid and erect.
Sperm exits the penis through ejaculation, the forceful expulsion of sperm out of a male's body.
After the semen is deposited in the female reproductive system, the sperm cells swim ultil they encounter the egg cell.
If any of the sperm does not penetrate the egg cell, fertilization does not occur.
On average, about 3.5 milliliters of semen is expelled during ejaculation.
It normally contains 300 to 400 million sperm cells, or about 5 to 10 million in just one drop.
Only a few reach the egg since most sperm die in the acidic environment of the female reproductive tract.
